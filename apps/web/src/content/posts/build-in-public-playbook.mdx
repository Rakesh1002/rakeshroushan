I have over 10,000 followers across Twitter/X and LinkedIn. I didn't get there by posting engagement bait, motivational quotes, or hot takes about AI replacing everyone's job.

I got there by **shipping products and telling people about it.**

That's the entire strategy. But the execution matters enormously. Most "build in public" attempts fail because people treat it as content marketing when it's actually a **distribution flywheel**. Done right, it creates a self-reinforcing loop: build, share, attract, collaborate, build more.

Done wrong, it's just screaming into the void.

This is the complete playbook.

## Why Build in Public Works (The Theory)

Building in public works because it exploits three psychological principles that paid advertising can't touch:

<div class="not-prose my-8 space-y-4">
  <div class="flex gap-4 p-5 rounded-xl border border-border bg-bg-card/50">
    <span class="text-2xl font-bold text-accent shrink-0">01</span>
    <div>
      <h4 class="font-semibold text-text mb-1">The IKEA Effect</h4>
      <p class="text-sm text-text-muted leading-relaxed">People value things more when they feel involved in building them. When your audience watches you build something from day zero, they feel co-ownership. They root for you. They tell their friends. They become customers not because of your marketing — because of the relationship.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="flex gap-4 p-5 rounded-xl border border-border bg-bg-card/50">
    <span class="text-2xl font-bold text-accent shrink-0">02</span>
    <div>
      <h4 class="font-semibold text-text mb-1">Social Proof via Process</h4>
      <p class="text-sm text-text-muted leading-relaxed">A polished landing page says "trust us." A public build log showing commits, decisions, pivots, and user conversations says "here's the evidence." The process <em>is</em> the proof. Nobody fakes a 12-month build log.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="flex gap-4 p-5 rounded-xl border border-border bg-bg-card/50">
    <span class="text-2xl font-bold text-accent shrink-0">03</span>
    <div>
      <h4 class="font-semibold text-text mb-1">Compounding Distribution</h4>
      <p class="text-sm text-text-muted leading-relaxed">Every build-in-public post is a permanent piece of content that can be found, shared, and referenced forever. A tweet from 6 months ago still drives traffic today. Ad spend stops working the second you stop paying. Content compounds.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

## The Viral Loop: How Building in Public Compounds

This is the core engine. Every step feeds the next:

<div class="not-prose my-8 p-6 rounded-xl border border-green-500/20 bg-green-500/5">
  <p class="text-sm font-semibold text-green-400 mb-4 text-center">The Build-in-Public Flywheel</p>

    <div class="p-3 rounded-lg bg-bg-card border border-border">
      <p class="font-medium text-text">1. Build</p>
      <p class="text-xs text-text-muted mt-1">Ship a feature, fix a bug, launch a product</p>
    </div>
    <div class="p-3 rounded-lg bg-bg-card border border-border">
      <p class="font-medium text-text">2. Share</p>
      <p class="text-xs text-text-muted mt-1">Post the journey: what, why, how, what you learned</p>
    </div>
    <div class="p-3 rounded-lg bg-bg-card border border-border">
      <p class="font-medium text-text">3. Attract</p>
      <p class="text-xs text-text-muted mt-1">Developers, designers, users find your content</p>
    </div>
    <div class="p-3 rounded-lg bg-bg-card border border-border">
      <p class="font-medium text-text">4. Collaborate</p>
      <p class="text-xs text-text-muted mt-1">Feedback, beta testers, contributors, customers</p>
    </div>
    <div class="p-3 rounded-lg bg-green-500/10 border border-green-500/30">
      <p class="font-medium text-green-400">5. Build More</p>
      <p class="text-xs text-text-muted mt-1">Feedback shapes the product, new stories to share</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <p class="text-xs text-green-400 text-center mt-4">Step 5 feeds back into Step 1. The loop compounds.</p>
</div>

The key insight: **building in public isn't a separate activity from building your product.** It IS your go-to-market. The sharing IS the distribution. The audience IS the beta test. If you separate "building" from "sharing," you'll burn out doing both. Integrate them into one workflow.

### What NOT to Share

<div class="not-prose my-8 grid md:grid-cols-2 gap-4">
  <div class="p-5 rounded-xl border border-red-500/20 bg-red-500/5">
    <p class="text-xs font-semibold text-red-400 uppercase tracking-wider mb-2">Never share</p>
    <div class="space-y-2 text-sm text-text-muted">
      <p>Exact revenue numbers when very early (looks like bragging about nothing)</p>
      <p>Customer data, even anonymized (breaks trust)</p>
      <p>Internal pricing strategy before launch</p>
      <p>Technical vulnerabilities or security issues</p>
      <p>Complaints about specific customers</p>
      <p>Your system prompts (competitors will copy them)</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="p-5 rounded-xl border border-green-500/20 bg-green-500/5">
    <p class="text-xs font-semibold text-green-400 uppercase tracking-wider mb-2">Always share</p>
    <div class="space-y-2 text-sm text-text-muted">
      <p>Percentage growth ("Revenue up 40% MoM")</p>
      <p>Aggregated user behavior patterns</p>
      <p>Technical architecture decisions and tradeoffs</p>
      <p>Failures and what you learned from them</p>
      <p>The "why" behind product decisions</p>
      <p>Screenshots and demos of new features</p>

## The 7 Mistakes I Made (So You Don't Have To)

<div class="not-prose my-8 space-y-4">
  <div class="p-5 rounded-xl border border-red-500/20 bg-red-500/5">
    <div class="flex items-start gap-3">
      <span class="text-lg font-bold text-red-400 shrink-0">1</span>
      <div>
        <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Posting only when things go well</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">For the first 3 months, I only shared wins. "Launched this!" "Hit this milestone!" Nobody engaged because it felt like a press release, not a journey. The moment I started sharing failures — products I killed, features that flopped, decisions I regretted — engagement tripled. Vulnerability is magnetic.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="p-5 rounded-xl border border-red-500/20 bg-red-500/5">
    <div class="flex items-start gap-3">
      <span class="text-lg font-bold text-red-400 shrink-0">2</span>
      <div>
        <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Cross-posting identical content</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">I used to copy-paste the same post to Twitter and LinkedIn. Twitter audience wants short, punchy, technical. LinkedIn audience wants professional framing with broader business context. Same story, different language. Adapt, don't copy.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="p-5 rounded-xl border border-red-500/20 bg-red-500/5">
    <div class="flex items-start gap-3">
      <span class="text-lg font-bold text-red-400 shrink-0">3</span>
      <div>
        <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Not including visuals</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">Text-only posts get 50-70% fewer impressions than posts with screenshots, videos, or diagrams. A 30-second screen recording of a feature you just shipped outperforms a 500-word description of it every single time. Show, don't tell.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="p-5 rounded-xl border border-red-500/20 bg-red-500/5">
    <div class="flex items-start gap-3">
      <span class="text-lg font-bold text-red-400 shrink-0">4</span>
      <div>
        <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Treating it as a chore instead of a habit</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">I tried "content batching" — sitting down on Sunday and writing a week's worth of posts. It felt forced and the content was generic. Now, I post immediately after building something, while the context and excitement are fresh. The best BIP content is written in the 10 minutes after you ship.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="p-5 rounded-xl border border-red-500/20 bg-red-500/5">
    <div class="flex items-start gap-3">
      <span class="text-lg font-bold text-red-400 shrink-0">5</span>
      <div>
        <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Ignoring DMs and replies</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">Build-in-public is a conversation, not a broadcast. For every post, spend equal time replying to comments, answering DMs, and engaging with others' BIP journeys. The relationships you build in DMs are worth 10x the followers you gain from posts. Some of my best beta testers and collaborators started as DM conversations.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="p-5 rounded-xl border border-red-500/20 bg-red-500/5">
    <div class="flex items-start gap-3">
      <span class="text-lg font-bold text-red-400 shrink-0">6</span>
      <div>
        <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Being too vague to be useful</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">"Working on an exciting new feature!" is a useless post. Nobody cares about your excitement. They care about specifics: what the feature is, why you're building it, what technical challenge you faced, what you learned. Specificity is the difference between content that gets shared and content that gets scrolled past.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="p-5 rounded-xl border border-red-500/20 bg-red-500/5">
    <div class="flex items-start gap-3">
      <span class="text-lg font-bold text-red-400 shrink-0">7</span>
      <div>
        <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Optimizing for followers instead of customers</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">I spent a month chasing viral content — hot takes, memes, engagement bait. Followers went up. Signups went flat. The audience I attracted wasn't my customer. When I switched back to genuine build content, follower growth slowed but signups and revenue doubled. Followers are a vanity metric. Signups are a business metric.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

## The Uncomfortable Truth About Build-in-Public

Here's the part nobody talks about:

<div class="not-prose my-8 space-y-4">
  <div class="flex gap-4 p-5 rounded-xl border border-yellow-500/20 bg-yellow-500/5">
    <span class="text-2xl font-bold text-yellow-400 shrink-0">01</span>
    <div>
      <h4 class="font-semibold text-text mb-1">The first 3 months are brutal</h4>
      <p class="text-sm text-text-muted leading-relaxed">You'll post to 50 followers. You'll get 2 likes. You'll wonder if anyone cares. They don't — yet. The first 1,000 followers are the hardest. It's a cold start problem. The only way through is consistency. I posted 3x/week for 12 weeks before anything felt like it was working.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="flex gap-4 p-5 rounded-xl border border-yellow-500/20 bg-yellow-500/5">
    <span class="text-2xl font-bold text-yellow-400 shrink-0">02</span>
    <div>
      <h4 class="font-semibold text-text mb-1">You MUST actually be building something</h4>
      <p class="text-sm text-text-muted leading-relaxed">Build-in-public without the "build" is just public. Nobody wants to follow someone who talks about building but ships nothing. The content IS the building. If you're not shipping features weekly, you don't have content. The solution isn't to write more — it's to build more.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="flex gap-4 p-5 rounded-xl border border-yellow-500/20 bg-yellow-500/5">
    <span class="text-2xl font-bold text-yellow-400 shrink-0">03</span>
    <div>
      <h4 class="font-semibold text-text mb-1">Competitors will see everything</h4>
      <p class="text-sm text-text-muted leading-relaxed">Yes, building in public means competitors know what you're working on. I've had competitors copy features I posted about within weeks. Here's why I still do it: the audience you build, the trust you earn, and the feedback loop you create are worth 100x more than the secrecy of a feature that anyone can build anyway. In AI especially, features are commodities. Distribution is the moat.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

## The 90-Day Quick Start Plan

If you're starting from zero, here's the exact plan I'd follow:

<div class="not-prose my-8 space-y-3">
  <div class="flex gap-4 p-4 rounded-xl border border-accent/20 bg-accent/5">
    <span class="text-lg font-bold text-accent shrink-0">Week 1-2</span>
    <div>
      <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Setup and first posts</p>
      <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">Optimize your Twitter/LinkedIn bio (what you're building + why). Write a "Day 0" post explaining what you're building and why. Share your first demo screenshot. Engage with 10 other builders daily — reply thoughtfully to their posts.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="flex gap-4 p-4 rounded-xl border border-accent/20 bg-accent/5">
    <span class="text-lg font-bold text-accent shrink-0">Week 3-4</span>
    <div>
      <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Find your rhythm</p>
      <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">Post 3x/week minimum. Use the templates above. Share one behind-the-scenes technical post, one demo, and one lesson learned. Track which posts get the most saves and link clicks (not likes — saves and clicks).</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="flex gap-4 p-4 rounded-xl border border-accent/20 bg-accent/5">
    <span class="text-lg font-bold text-accent shrink-0">Week 5-8</span>
    <div>
      <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Double down on what works</p>
      <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">By now you'll see patterns — certain content types resonate more than others. Double down. Write your first long-form blog post from a thread that performed well. Start cross-posting to LinkedIn with adapted framing. Launch on Product Hunt or Hacker News and document the experience publicly.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="flex gap-4 p-4 rounded-xl border border-accent/20 bg-accent/5">
    <span class="text-lg font-bold text-accent shrink-0">Week 9-12</span>
    <div>
      <p class="font-semibold text-text text-sm mb-1">Build the flywheel</p>
      <p class="text-xs text-text-muted">You should have 200-500 followers who care about your journey. Start converting followers to users — share product links with context ("just shipped X, try it free"). Ask for feedback publicly. Feature users/collaborators in your posts. The flywheel should start self-sustaining around week 10-12.</p>
    </div>

## The Bottom Line

<div class="not-prose my-8 p-6 rounded-xl border border-accent/20 bg-accent/5">
  <p class="text-lg font-semibold text-text mb-3">Build-in-public isn't a marketing strategy. It's a shipping strategy that happens to also be marketing.</p>
  <p class="text-sm text-text-muted leading-relaxed">The best build-in-public accounts don't "create content." They <strong class="text-text">build things</strong> and <strong class="text-text">tell people about it</strong>. That's it. The content is a byproduct of the building, not the other way around. If you're spending more time on your posts than on your product, you've got it backwards. Ship first. Share second. The audience will come — not because you're a great writer, but because you're a great builder who also writes.</p>
</div>

I didn't get 10K followers by being clever on the internet. I got them by shipping 12 products in 12 months and telling people what I learned along the way. The products are the content. The journey is the brand. Everything else is noise.

Start building. Start sharing. The followers are a lagging indicator of the work.
